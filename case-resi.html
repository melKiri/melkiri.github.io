The brief

A Netflix-style media library.
Built for the church context.

Resi is Pushpay's live streaming and video product — a separate platform with its own team, design system, and roadmap. The opportunity was to build an on-demand content experience that would let churches offer their congregants a branded, distraction-free place to watch past services, sermon series, and media content.

The challenge wasn't just building a media player. It was designing an experience that felt native across three very different surfaces — a standalone product (Resi On Demand / Media Sites), an embedded feature inside the Church App, and an admin configuration flow inside App Studio.

"No redirects. No competing algorithms. No distractions. One destination where your audience can watch live and explore past content — all in one place."

I was the sole designer on the end-user experience, working directly with the Resi product and engineering team — while simultaneously maintaining my work in the App Studio and Church App space.

The work

Same product.
Three design problems.

Each surface had a different user, a different context, and a different definition of success. The design had to work for all three without compromising any of them.

Surface 01 · End user

Resi On Demand

The core viewing experience for congregants. A branded, searchable content library where live streams auto-save and on-demand content is organised into playlists and series. Designed to feel familiar — like a streaming service — while remaining distraction-free and entirely owned by the church.

  • Browse, discover, and watch on-demand content
  • Playlist and series organisation
  • Live + on-demand unified in one destination
  • Branded experience — no third-party interference

Surface 02 · Church App

In-app integration

How Resi On Demand lives inside the Church App — the white-label app used by congregants. The challenge here was integration without the seam showing. Content needed to feel native to the app, not like a webview dropped in. Congregants shouldn't feel like they've left the app to watch a video.

  • Seamless embed within Church App navigation
  • Consistent visual language across both products
  • Minimal friction from app to content

Surface 03 · Admin

App Studio config

The admin-facing configuration flow in App Studio — how church admins connect their Resi account, set up their media library, and embed it into their app. Required close collaboration with the App Studio engineering team to design a setup flow that was clear enough for non-technical admins to complete without support.

  • Resi account connection and authentication
  • Media library configuration
  • Embed and publish flow

Design challenges

Working across
two product systems.

Designing across Resi and Pushpay meant navigating two different design systems, two engineering teams, and two different product roadmaps — simultaneously. Every decision had downstream implications on at least two surfaces.

01
Two design systems, one coherent experience. Resi had its own visual language. The Church App had Prism. The integration had to feel native in both without creating a third, undefined aesthetic.
02
Different users, shared content. Admins configure what congregants see. Every admin decision has a direct end-user consequence — which meant designing both sides with that dependency always in mind.
03
Familiar but not generic. The "Netflix-style" reference set expectations. The design needed to feel intuitive — leveraging familiar patterns — without looking like it was just copying a consumer streaming app for a church context.

What shipped

Live, branded,
distraction-free.

Resi On Demand shipped as a fully branded, searchable content destination — unifying live and on-demand content in one place. Churches could finally offer their congregants a streaming experience they owned entirely, without competing algorithms or third-party interference pulling viewers away.

The Church App integration brought that experience directly into the app congregants already used for giving, groups, and events — making media consumption part of the existing community experience rather than a separate destination.

The App Studio configuration flow gave admins a clear, self-serve path to set up and embed their media library — reducing dependency on technical support for what should be a straightforward task.
