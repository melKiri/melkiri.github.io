The starting point

The team everyone
underestimated.

App Studio wasn't the glamorous product squad. It was a small team, often overlooked in company-wide conversations, sitting at the intersection of every other product without being fully claimed by any of them.

When I joined, end-user knowledge was almost nonexistent. We had reasonable understanding of church admins — the people who built the app — but the congregants actually using the Church App were largely a blind spot. No structured research, limited usability data, and a design system that reflected the problem: hardcoded values, components built in isolation, nothing truly reusable.

"We were building products for people we barely knew, using tools that didn't talk to each other, in a team that had to earn its seat at every table."

I chose to see this as an opportunity. Being at the edges of the product meant I had to understand everything — Giving, ChMS, Resi, the admin experience, the end-user experience. That forced cross-product literacy became the foundation of how I think about design systems today.

Research foundation

Advocating for the
people we didn't know.

One of the first things I pushed for was bringing more end-user focus to App Studio — not just admin knowledge. Pushpay had a UX Research team, and I worked closely with them to advocate for and conduct research that the team hadn't previously prioritised: usability testing, interviews, and synthesis that we could share across the wider UX org.

The Church App is where community lives for congregants — events, groups, giving, media, communication. These are people building a sense of belonging through a digital product. That context matters enormously for design decisions, and we couldn't make good ones without understanding who we were designing for.

Deep-dive

All work › App Studio › Deep-dive: StudioC

StudioC, up close.
One feature, two user types, three measures of success.

StudioC brings a third-party personalisation model into the Church App. The auth model available at the time meant log-in happens on the church's side — so I designed a pragmatic pattern around that constraint: anonymous content shows by default, with an invitation to log in, not a wall.

"Admin configures it once. Congregant sees it every time they open the app."

User type 01 — Church admins

Configuring StudioC inside App Studio

StudioC slots into the Integrations panel as a new draggable — same authoring model admins already knew. What they set in Home Settings becomes what congregants see. Title, description and CTA are configured once, with a live mobile preview before publish.

How we measured success

Three axes, three stakeholders

One-sided metrics flatter. Three axes kept us honest.

Admin experience

Setup confidence

Can a church admin configure it without support?

Congregant adoption

Invitation-to-action

Does the soft log-in pattern convert better than a wall?

Product impact

Engagement signal

Does personalised content change behaviour, or just move it?

Pattern worth noting

Why this pattern travels.

A content model where admins configure once and end-users experience the result — anonymous-by-default with a soft invitation — is the kind that invites progression mechanics. Unlocking content as users move through a personalised journey. A direction I'd explore in any content-driven product, not just this one.

The unexpected outcome

The "overlooked" team
taught me the whole platform.

Working at the intersection of Giving, ChMS, Resi, and the app layer meant I had to develop fluency across the entire Pushpay portfolio. I couldn't design the Events experience without understanding ChMS. I couldn't design the media experience without understanding Resi. I couldn't design the giving experience without understanding the Donor Portal.

That forced breadth is exactly what made me effective when the opportunity came to lead Design System work. You can't build a system for 7 products if you only understand 1.

Currently in progress

The debt we found then.
The work we're fixing now.

The inconsistencies I encountered in App Studio — hardcoded values, components built in isolation, no shared token architecture — weren't unique to that team. They were systemic.

As Design System Lead, I'm now leading an atom-level audit across the full UI library and component library (Storybook). The premise: get the atoms right, and every molecule and organism built on top of them benefits automatically — without blocking other teams or creating dependencies.

I'm using Claude to run cross-channel audits between Figma, Storybook, and GitHub — identifying variable mismatches, hardcoded values, and component health — then generating Jira tickets directly from the findings. Work that would take weeks of manual audit now surfaces in hours.

"By working hard on atoms today, any molecules and organisms built by other teams will benefit seamlessly — without us becoming a blocker."
